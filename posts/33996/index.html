Understanding the LDA Algorithm

Edited on 2022-05-30 In ML > Algorithms ‰∏≠ÊñáÁâà

The Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) algorithm is a text mining algorithm that aims to extract topics from long texts. In a nutshell, LDA assumes that each document defines a distribution over topics, and each topic defines a distribution over words. Each word is generated by first sampling a topic from the document, and then sampling a word from the topic. To train an LDA is to solve for the parameters of these two distributions (doc-topic and topic-word) given many documents; To evaluate an LDA usually means predicting the topic distribution for a new unseen document.

This article will introduce LDA in a top-down fashion: Starting with the NeurIPS paper example, it first formulates topic modeling as a parameter estimation problem; then comes some essential math tools (e.g. Dirichlet distribution, Gibbs sampling, etc.); and finally, derives the LDA training and testing algorithms.

The Dirichlet distribution 101

Prior, data, and posterior

Let‚Äôs take a closer look at the parameters we want to solve: the topic distribution for each of the M documents (Œò\mathbf \ThetaŒò) and the word distribution for each of the K topics (Œ¶\mathbf \PhiŒ¶). In order to estimate them, the LDA algorithm makes some assumptions about their prior. What is a prior? It‚Äôs our belief about the parameters without observing the data. In contrast, after you observe the data, you get the posterior. Usually we are interested in the posterior, since it incorporates the information of data and therefore is good for estimating the parameters using methods like MAP (maximum-a-posteriori) or EAP (expectated-a-posteriori).

Here‚Äôs an example: Imagine you have a coin which you believe to be a fair coin, so you may think its probability of showing head is most likely around 0.5 (prior P(pcoin)P(p_{\text{coin}})P(pcoin‚Äã)). However, after tossing it several times, it always shows head (data). Therefore your belief about its head probability gradually shifts from 0.5 to very close to 1 as the evidence accumulates (posterior P(pcoin‚à£data)P(p_{\text{coin}}\mid \text{data})P(pcoin‚Äã‚à£data)). In this example, both the prior and the posterior of the coin‚Äôs probability can be nicely modeled with the Beta distribution. We can either report the expectation or the maximum of the posterior to answer the question ‚ÄúWhat do you think is the probability of coin after observing several tosses?‚Äù

Note that in the above two examples, the Beta/Dirichlet distribution aren‚Äôt our only choice. The Dirichlet Distribution: Jar of dices

We‚Äôve been using sloppy terms like ‚Äúbelief‚Äù to describe the following intuitions:

  1. A prior/posterior is a distribution that models the probability density of the estimated parameter. If we make the analogy that the topic distribution of a paper (a.k.a the ‚Äútheme‚Äù) is like a K-sided dice, then the Dirichlet distribution is just like a jar of dices. Each time you reach in (which we call ‚Äúsampling‚Äù), you get a new dice. In the paper example, before you observe the data, the jar is considered to be more likely to give you a fair dice; After you observe the data, the jar is considered to be more likely to give you a biased dice.
  2. The expectation of the posterior shifts as you accumulate more data. This is roughly equivalent to sampling from the jar many times and taking the average of the dices you get.

Now let‚Äôs take a closer look at the Dirichlet distribution, our jar of dices.

Recall that, a ‚Äúdice‚Äù refers to a multinomial distribution Mult(p1,...,pn)\text{Mult}(p_1,...,p_n)Mult(p1‚Äã,...,pn‚Äã) with ‚àëi=1npi=1\sum_{i=1}^n p_i=1‚àëi=1n‚Äãpi‚Äã=1 and pi‚â•0p_i \geq 0pi‚Äã‚â•0. And remember our ultimate goal is to estimate the dice by solving for the posterior, which is the jar after observing some data.

So, what is the data? Intuitively, the best way to collect data in order to estimate a dice, is to toss it many times and count the occurrences of each outcome. Here‚Äôs an example: Imagine you toss a three-sided doc-topic dice 100 times, and this is what you get:

  • Vision: 50 (50%)
  • Language: 40 (40%)
  • Graph: 10 (10%)

And suppose your prior is that the dice should be fair. The most naive way to incorporate this belief, is to add a ‚Äúpseudo-count‚Äù to your observation. The amount of ‚Äúpseudo-count‚Äù depends on the strength of your prior: If you really think the dice should be as fair as possible despite the observation, then you probably want to add a large pseudo-count (e.g. 100) to your data, which pulls the expectation of the posterior much closer to a fair dice:

  • Vision: 50+100 (37.5%)
  • Language: 40+100 (35%)
  • Graph: 10+100 (27.5%)

Otherwise you may want to add a tiny bit of pseudo-count (e.g. 1), which perturbs the observation by a small amount:

  • Vision: 50+1 (49.5%)
  • Language: 40+1 (39.8%)
  • Graph: 10+1 (10.7%)

The problem is that we don‚Äôt really know which topic is behind each word! If you are familiar with the EM algorithm, you‚Äôd probably know this is called a ‚Äúhidden variable‚Äù. LDA Training

Recall that, as mentioned at the beginning of this article, the goal of LDA training is to estimate Œò\mathbf \ThetaŒò and Œ¶\mathbf \PhiŒ¶ given the words we see, which requires sampling Z\mathbf ZZ from P(Z‚à£W)P(\mathbf Z \mid \mathbf W)P(Z‚à£W). Let‚Äôs do it with Gibbs sampling.

As you probably already noticed, the only tricky part is the (*) line, which means ‚ÄúFreezing the values of all the other dimensions, and sampling the current dimension according to their values.‚Äù For sampling Z\mathbf ZZ, this means ‚Äúfreezing the topics of all the other words, and sampling the topic of the current word.‚Äù This can be written as:
